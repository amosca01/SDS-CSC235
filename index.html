Course Overview and Policies

Description

Visual analytics is the science of combining interactive visual interfaces and information visualization techniques with automatic algorithms to support analytical reasoning through human-computer interaction. People use visual analytics tools and techniques to synthesize information and derive insight from massive, dynamic, ambiguous, and often conflicting data. This course will serve as an introduction to the science and technology of visual analytics and will include lectures on both theoretical foundations and application methodologies. The goals of this course are for students to (1) develop a comprehensive understanding of this emerging, multidisciplinary field, and (2) apply that understanding toward a focused research problem in a real-world application or a domain of personal interest.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students will be able to:
  • Critically evaluate inteactive data visualizations and suggest improvements and refinements
  • Apply a structured design process to create effective visualization tools
  • Conceptualize ideas and interaction techniques using sketching and prototyping
  • Use principles of human perception and cognition in visualization design
  • Develop interactive, data-driven visualizations programmed in HTML5/JS
  • Work effectively as part of a cross-discplinary project team

Topics Covered

The following topics will be covered in this class:
  • Visualizing big data and algorithms
  • Interaction theory
  • Bespoke visualization coding with D3.js
  • User-centered design and evaluation

Topics Not Covered

The following topics will not be covered in this class:
  • In-depth visualization basics (take SDS/CSC 109 for this)
  • Mental models (take SDS/CSC 109 for this)
  • Introduction to coding (take CSC 110 or 120 for this)
  • Introduction to statistics (take SDS 210 for this)
  • Data wrangling (take SDS 192 for this)
  • ML/AI (take a CSC or SDS elective for this)

Prerequisites

CSC 120 or equivalent. Students are expected to be intermediate-level coders, and to have experience with basic statistics and data wrangling. While not explicitly required, it is strongly recommended that students take CSC 210 and at least at least one other SDS class prior to CSC/SDS 235.

There are a variety of assignments in this class: Homeworks, Mid-Semester Project, Final Project, and Participation and Engagement. The purpose of this is provide as many ways as possible for students to demonstrate mastery of material.

Homeworks

Homework is a chance to practice topics covered in lecture and receive feedback. See rubrics for grading guidelines. During the first half of the semester, the primary deliverables for this course will be weekly data challenges. A new dataset will be introduced during lecture, along with a set of guiding questions. Your task is to use what you know about data visualization and modeling to wrangle, explore, and ultimately report back your findings. Along the way, you'll document your analysis process, justify your visualization choices, and report any assumptions you make about the data.

Mid-Semester Project

The mid-semester project is a chance for students to work together and engage with an existing visual analytic tool. Student will work in groups to prepare a short (~5min) case study of an existing visual analytics system: who built it, who it was designed for, how specific design choices shape the way the system works (or doesn't), and proposed changes or extensions for the tool.

Final Project

Final projects present the opportunity for students to work with real scientists to create a visual analytics tool that will assist with their research. Alternatively, students may apply the visualization techniques covered in class to a topic of interest to them. This assignment will be completed in groups. Grading will look at technical details, theoretical foundations, and creativity.

Participation and Engagement

Participation and engagement will take into account contributions in class which demonstrate intellectual curiosity or clear understanding of a topic, comments which help others in class to learn a difficult concept, timeliness and proactive communication about class attendance, and lab submissions.

Late Policy

All assignments are due as listed on the Course Schedule. Because our lives and learning do not always go as planned, every student will be able grant themselves extensions on homework assignments according to the instructions below.

Remember, we develop skills through constant practice. This course features weekly assignments, which build from one week to the next, and each assignment is designed to take a full week of work. Delaying the completion of an assignment will put you at a disadvantage going into the next one. Nevertheless, sometimes a little flexibility makes a huge difference, so students are empowered to give themselves extensions when it would aid their ability to master the material. Only extensions which precisely adhere to the guidelines below will be considered valid. Late assignments without a valid extension will receive no credit.

Extension Guidelines

Extensions must be requested before the work is due, and they will not be given retroactively. When working with a group, students submit a collective extension request. Work turned in under an extension will receive the lowest priority for grading, and in some cases may not be returned until the end of the semester.

Requesting an Extension

Prior to the original deadline for the assignment, submit a file called extension.txt (or .pdf) on Gradescope that contains the following:

  • Your name (and group members' names)
  • Assignment number and original due date
  • Duration of extension and new due date
  • Review of any prior extension requests

For example:

  Ab Mosca and Jordan Crouser
  HW09 originally due 10/01/24
  2 day extension, now due 10/03/2024
  Previous extentions: HW02 (1 day), HW7 (2 days)

Be sure that you do not submit any other files with your extension request. You can bring your code in progress to office or TA hours if you need. Do not turn in work that you do not want to be graded.

Once you’ve finished, and before your new deadline expires, submit all your files for the assignment as usual.

Changing an Extension

If you submit an extension.txt before the deadline and then decide you do not need it, just turn your code as usual and include an extension.txt file without any text. If you realize that you need even more time, you may grant yourself an additional extension following the same procedure again. However, repeated resets are unlikely to enhance your learning, so be mindful about how much time you add to your clock!

Revise and Resubmit

In order to improve your learning and reduce grade anxiety, you may revise and resubmit homework assignments in this class. The rules for revising and resubmitting assignments are as follows:

  • You may not resubmit an assignment on which you received a 0.
  • Your resubmission must include a changelog explaining or showing what changed from the first submission.
  • Revise and resubmits are unlimited.
  • The highest grade will prevail (even if it was not the most recent submission).
Resubmissions missing a changelog or tracked changes will not be graded.

Academic Integrity

Students are strongly encouraged to form study groups and to collaborate on assignments. The following information is required for all submitted work:

  • The names of all collaborating students be listed at the top of the submission. If you worked alone, please state: "I did not collaborate with anyone on this assignment."
  • A "References" section, with in-line citations to any resources you used. Citations should include page numbers (if a printed resource) or a direct URL (if an online resource). If you did not use any resources in completing the assignment, please state: "I did not utilize any external resources in completing this assignment."

You may consult online resources as part of your coursework, but you may not directly copy thoughts or code from online sources.

Generative AI may not be used to perform the intellectual tasks required to complete an assignment. In other words, your writing and code must be your own, not generated by AI (this includes modifying code or text initially generated by ChatGPT - doing so is a violation of academic integrity for this course).

You may use generative AI to help debug your code or as a spell check, provided you submit a PDF of your entire chat transcript with your assignment. Absence of said PDF transcript is a violation of academic integrity for this course.
